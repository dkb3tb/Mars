On a mission to Mars, a ship will travel for seven months, and hundreds of millions of miles through space. The trajectory will, at some point, need to be corrected in order for the astronauts to reach the red planet.

To correct the path of the orbiter, they will perform what is called a trajectory correction maneuver (TCM). Multiple TCMs may be necessary. The first and often largest maneuver removes injection errors and points the craft in the right direction. Subsequent maneuvers will remove errors from previous TCMs and other general erros in flight.

In performing a TCM, it's important for the system to be able to have a sense of direction. The Euler angles (Tait-Bryan angles when applied to aerodynamics) allow for representation of orientation on a mobile frame of reference.

The "roll", or angle of rotation about the horizontal axis, is in line with the primary direction of thrust. This parameter is important for altitude control.

Using the moment of inertia about the roll axis, as well as angular acceleration, Newton's second law can be rewritten as T = I*a. Using this formula, we are able to calculate the net torque exerted on a vehicle. With torque, the resulting force (thrust of one thruster) perpendicular to the roll axis can be derived.
